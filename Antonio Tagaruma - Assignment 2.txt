STAGE 2. Pedagogical and Assessment Design 
4. Learning objectives 
By the end of this comprehensive learning experience, students will be able to: 
●​ Technical Web Development Skills (Execution & Implementation): 
○​ Translate complex, AI-generated UI/UX layouts into fully functional, 
responsive web pages. 
○​ Demonstrate proficiency in structuring semantic HTML5 documents and 
applying advanced CSS3 styling (including Flexbox and CSS Grid) to ensure 
responsive layouts across mobile, tablet, and desktop devices. 
○​ Integrate basic JavaScript to create interactive DOM elements (such as 
responsive navigation menus, modal windows, or image carousels). 
●​ Advanced AI Literacy (Design & Coding Workflows): 
○​ Effectively utilize Google Stitch to rapidly generate, iterate upon, and refine 
complete design systems and interactive prototypes by engineering highly 
specific, context-aware text prompts. 
○​ Master the use of Google Antigravity, an AI-powered IDE, to delegate specific 
frontend coding tasks, debug syntax errors autonomously, and manage AI 
agents to assist in writing modular code. 
●​ Conceptual Knowledge (UX/UI & Environmental Awareness): 
○​ Understand and apply core principles of User Experience (UX) and User 
Interface (UI) design, including visual hierarchy, color theory, accessibility 
standards (WCAG), and user-centric navigation. 
○​ Articulate the real-world socio-economic and environmental impacts of 
sustainable tourism on the Ría de Arousa coastal ecosystem, translating this 
knowledge into compelling digital narratives. 
●​ Critical Application & Ethical AI Verification: 
○​ Critically analyze, verify, and edit AI-generated layouts and code to ensure 
they are free of algorithmic hallucinations, inefficiencies, or cultural biases. 
○​ Ensure that all digital content authentically, respectfully, and accurately 
represents the local maritime culture, traditions, and economic realities of the 
Illa de Arousa community. 
●​ Soft Skills & Professional Simulation: 
○​ Work collaboratively in a simulated "digital agency" environment, adopting 
specific professional roles (e.g., Project Manager, Lead UI Designer, Lead 
Developer). 
○​ Successfully execute a professional project handoff, transitioning seamlessly 
from the ideation and design phase in Google Stitch to the technical 
development phase in Google Antigravity. 
5. Pedagogical approach 
●​ Type of learning approach: The instructional foundation relies on a synthesis of 
AI-Assisted Project-Based Learning (PBL) and Place-Based Education. By 
roleplaying as a local digital agency commissioned by the municipality, students are 
immersed in a highly active, collaborative learning environment that mimics a 
modern, industry-standard tech workflow. First, they will use Google Stitch to rapidly 
prototype high-fidelity designs based on descriptive prompts and rough conceptual 
wireframes. Subsequently, they will utilize Google Antigravity's agent-first paradigm 
to bring those static designs to life as functional code. Crucially, this entire workflow is 
grounded in their local geographic reality, transforming abstract IT concepts into a 
tangible tool for community empowerment. 
●​ How technology supports learning: Technology and Artificial Intelligence serve not 
merely as passive tools, but as active co-creators and cognitive scaffolds in this 
learning design. 
○​ TPACK Framework Integration: This project tightly weaves the three 
domains of TPACK. It requires Technological Knowledge (the ability to 
operate and prompt specialized AI design tools like Google Stitch and 
complex coding platforms like Google Antigravity). It demands Content 
Knowledge (understanding the principles of sustainable tourism and the 
historical context of local maritime heritage). Finally, it leverages Pedagogical 
Knowledge (using PBL, simulated agency workflows, and peer-to-peer 
scaffolding to facilitate deep, active learning). 
○​ SAMR Model Application: The use of generative AI in this context propels 
the learning experience directly to the "Redefinition" level of the SAMR model. 
Traditional web development courses for 4th-year ESO students often get 
bogged down in the steep learning curve of syntax, leading to frustration and 
blank-page anxiety. By utilizing AI tools as "co-pilots," students bypass these 
initial technical barriers. They can achieve professional-looking prototypes 
and complex site functionality that would normally require years of specialized 
training. This redefines the task: rather than focusing solely on memorizing 
code syntax, students are elevated to the role of "directors" and "editors," 
focusing heavily on computational logic, problem-solving, architectural design, 
and critical thinking. 
6. Assessment strategy 
●​ Type of assessment: The project will utilize a robust, multi-faceted mixed 
assessment strategy to evaluate both the process (how they work) and the final 
product (what they build). 
1.​ Formative Assessment: Continuous and embedded throughout the daily 
workflow, focusing on the iterative process of prompting, designing, and 
coding. 
2.​ Summative Assessment: Occurs at the culmination of the project when the 
final, fully functional websites are "launched" and formally evaluated against a 
rubric. 
3.​ Peer 
Assessment: 
Structurally integrated as a "quality assurance" 
mechanism, particularly during the critical "handoff" phase between UI/UX 
design and backend coding. 
●​ Feedback methods: 
1.​ Continuous Teacher Feedback (The "Technical Director" Role): The instructor 
will step back from traditional lecturing to act as a "Technical Director" or 
agency mentor. They will provide weekly, targeted formative feedback during 
lab sessions, focusing specifically on how responsibly, ethically, and 
effectively students are engineering their prompts for the AI tools. 
2.​ Peer Design Handoff & Usability Testing: Before writing a single line of code, 
teams will present their interactive Google Stitch-generated UI/UX prototypes 
to a rival team. The opposing team will conduct constructive "user testing" 
and provide formal feedback on usability, accessibility, and cultural accuracy. 
Only after this feedback is integrated will the original team export the frontend 
code for refinement in Google Antigravity. 
●​ Assessment tool or strategy: A comprehensive, four-dimensional Project Rubric 
will serve as the primary evaluation tool. It will assess the following areas equally: 
1.​ AI Tool Application & Prompt Engineering (25%): Evaluates the sophistication, 
iteration, and purposefulness of the team's prompts within Google Stitch and 
Google Antigravity, assessed via a required "Prompt Log" journal submitted 
by each team. 
2.​ Technical Execution & Code Architecture (25%): Evaluates the cleanliness, 
semantic structure, and responsiveness of the final HTML/CSS/JS code, with 
specific attention to how well the AI-generated code was customized, 
debugged, and manually integrated by the human students. 
3.​ Design, UX, and Usability (25%): Evaluates the website's intuitive navigation, 
visual appeal, accessibility compliance, and how faithfully the final coded site 
matches the initial approved Google Stitch prototype. 
4.​ Content, Context, and Cultural Authenticity (25%): Evaluates the depth of 
research, the persuasive quality of the copy, and how effectively the website 
promotes sustainable tourism while truthfully reflecting the authentic maritime 
identity of Illa de Arousa.
