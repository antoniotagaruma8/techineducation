STAGE 3. Learning Experience and Implementation 
7.​ Learning activity design 
Describe the activity step by step: 
Phase 1: The Agency Briefing & Discovery (Week 1) 
●​ What students do: Students form "digital agencies" of 3-4 
members. They receive a mock creative brief from the Illa de 
Arousa municipality requesting a web portal for sustainable 
maritime tourism. They conduct online and offline research about 
the Ría de Arousa ecosystem and local traditions. 
●​ How it works: This phase sets the real-world context. Students 
must define the target audience and identify key cultural elements 
to highlight (e.g., the batea industry, local lighthouses). 
Phase 2: Rapid Prototyping with Google Stitch (Weeks 2-3) 
●​ What students do: Teams translate their research into UI/UX 
wireframes. They use text-to-design prompts in Google Stitch to 
generate layout concepts, iterating based on usability principles. 
●​ How it works: Instead of drawing boxes, students practice "prompt 
engineering," learning how to command the AI to create 
accessible, culturally appropriate designs. At the end of Week 3, 
teams conduct a "Design Handoff," critiquing a rival team's 
prototype. 
Phase 3: AI-Assisted Development with Google Antigravity (Weeks 
4-5) 
●​ What students do: Teams export their approved UI components 
and transition into the Google Antigravity IDE. They act as lead 
developers, directing AI agents to generate the HTML, CSS, and 
basic JavaScript required to make the site functional and 
responsive. 
●​ How it works: The focus shifts from syntax memorization to 
computational logic and debugging. Students must read the 
AI-generated code, verify its functionality, fix responsive design 
breaks, and integrate their specific cultural text and images. 
Phase 4: Launch and Retrospective (Week 6) 
●​ What students do: Teams finalize their code, conduct cross-device 
testing, and present their final web portals to the class. 
●​ How it works: Each presentation must justify their design choices, 
explain their AI prompting strategy, and defend how their site 
actively promotes the sustainability of Illa de Arousa. 
8.​ Use of technology and resources 
Explain the resources needed: 
●​ Digital tools, platforms, or apps: 
1.​ Google Stitch: Used for rapid UI/UX ideation and generating 
high-fidelity design systems from text prompts. 
2.​ Google Antigravity: The AI-powered IDE where students will 
manage coding agents to write, debug, and structure the 
HTML/CSS/JS frontend code. 
3.​ Collaborative Docs (e.g., Google Workspace): For managing the 
project timeline, drafting site copy, and logging AI prompts. 
●​ Physical materials or classroom setup: A computer lab equipped 
with reliable internet access. Ideally, dual-monitor setups (one 
screen for the AI IDE, one for the browser preview) to mimic a 
professional developer environment. Whiteboards for initial 
brainstorming. 
●​ Justification of tool selection: Using Google Stitch and Antigravity 
completely transforms the learning curve. Traditionally, 4th-year 
ESO students would spend weeks just learning basic HTML tags, 
resulting in very basic, visually unappealing websites. By 
leveraging generative AI, the cognitive load shifts from 
"memorizing syntax" to "system architecture, prompt engineering, 
and critical design thinking." This allows teenagers to produce 
professional-grade artifacts that genuinely reflect their community, 
highly increasing their intrinsic motivation while teaching them the 
actual tools modern developers use. 
9.​ Implementation plan 
Explain how the activity would work in practice: 
●​ Classroom structure: The classroom operates as a "Tech Hub" or 
co-working space rather than a traditional lecture hall. Students sit 
in pods with their agency teams, fostering constant collaboration, 
pair-programming, and real-time problem solving. 
●​ Teacher role: The teacher shifts from "lecturer" to 
"Technical/Creative Director." Rather than standing at the front of 
the room, the teacher circulates among the agencies. Their 
primary job is to unblock teams struggling with AI prompts, enforce 
coding best practices, ensure ethical AI usage, and push students 
to critically evaluate if the AI's output aligns with their cultural 
goals. 
●​ Student roles: Within each agency, students assume rotating 
professional roles: 
1.​ Project Manager: Tracks deadlines and ensures the team stays 
aligned with the municipality brief. 
2.​ Lead UX Designer: Owns the Google Stitch workflow and interface 
decisions. 
3.​ Lead Developer: Owns the Google Antigravity workflow, code 
integration, and debugging. 
4.​ Content Strategist: Ensures the local maritime culture is accurately 
represented in the text and media. 
●​ Adaptation to the chosen context: Because Illa de Arousa is an 
island context that may face occasional connectivity dips, the 
implementation plan includes "offline fallback" activities, such as 
sketching wireframes on paper or writing site copy offline. 
Furthermore, the project explicitly taps into the students' lived 
reality. They are encouraged to bring in photos they took of the 
local ports or conduct informal interviews with family members in 
the fishing industry to populate their websites, ensuring the digital 
output is deeply rooted in their physical community.
